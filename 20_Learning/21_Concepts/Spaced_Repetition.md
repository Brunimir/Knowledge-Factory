## Core Idea (Summary)
Spaced repetition involves revisiting information at increasing intervals over time to prevent the brain from clearing it as unused. It is like building a brick wall — you must let the mortar dry between layers or the structure collapses. Review a new concept 10 minutes after learning it, then two days later, then a week, then a month.

## Mechanism (Key details)
- Prevents the natural fading of neural traces by triggering reconsolidation at the moment the memory is about to decay — each review extends and strengthens the trace.
- Leverages the sleep-based reconsolidation process: each spaced review followed by sleep receives an additional consolidation cycle.
- Moves information from working memory toward long-term storage through repeated retrieval at increasing intervals, not through massed repetition.
- Tools like Anki implement this algorithmically by scheduling reviews based on self-reported difficulty, allocating more time to weak items.
- **Boundary condition:** Spaced repetition requires time between sessions to work — cramming the night before a test eliminates the interval, eliminating the benefit. It is structurally incompatible with last-minute learning.

## Application
- **When to use:** Critical for memorising facts, formulas, or concepts that must be retained for months or years — languages, medical knowledge, technical frameworks.
- **When NOT to use:** Not suitable for cramming immediately before a deadline; the neural structures need the interval to solidify, and without it the method provides no advantage over massed practice.
