## Core Idea (Summary)
The mind does not process information word by word — it groups incoming data into spatial, relational images to manage cognitive load. Understanding something means converting it into a simplified visual map of how the ideas relate, not storing a transcript of the words. Reading a map versus reading a list of street names is the difference.

## Mechanism (Key details)
- The mind forms spatial images from groups of words, not individual tokens; dense unbroken prose is harder to retain than structured text because it gives the clustering mechanism nothing to grip.
- Relational vocabulary — words like spread, excess, reduction, above — is more cognitively efficient than raw numbers because it encodes position and relationship, not just quantity.
- Effective mental images are skeletal, capturing the structure of relationships rather than photographic detail; over-precision in a mental image can actually hinder comprehension.
- **Boundary condition:** Cluster processing breaks down when the relationships between items are genuinely non-spatial — pure chronological lists, arbitrary enumerations. Forcing a spatial metaphor onto non-spatial data creates a false image that misleads rather than clarifies.

## Application
- **When to use:** In instructional design, strategic planning, or any communication where the goal is understanding rather than information transfer — ask whether the audience can form a mental image of how the ideas relate.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not rely on cluster processing for purely aesthetic or emotional communication where structure matters less than feeling, or when the audience needs exact figures rather than relational understanding.
