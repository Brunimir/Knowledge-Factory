## Core Idea (Summary)
Interleaving is the practice of mixing different types of problems or topics within one study session. A professional athlete who practises different shots in random order rather than doing 100 identical layups builds the ability to select the right technique — not just execute a rehearsed one.

## Mechanism (Key details)
- Forces the brain to learn when to use a technique, not just how to execute it — building strategy-selection alongside strategy-execution.
- Builds flexibility by requiring the brain to jump between different neural patterns, preventing over-specialisation on one approach.
- Prevents overlearning the easy material by forcing deliberate practice on difficult transitions between problem types.
- **Boundary condition:** Interleaving can be overwhelming during the very first encounter with genuinely new material when a worked-through example is still needed. Apply it once a basic conceptual hook exists, not as the first exposure.

## Application
- **When to use:** Highly effective for subjects where selecting the right strategy matters as much as executing it — mathematics, science, languages, clinical diagnosis.
- **When NOT to use:** Not appropriate as the first exposure to a brand-new concept that has no existing mental hook to interleave with.
