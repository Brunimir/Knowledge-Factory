## Core Idea (Summary)
Chunking is the process of binding several small pieces of information into one meaningful packet the brain can handle as a single unit. It is like zipping a large folder — once compressed, it takes up one working-memory slot instead of seven, freeing space for more complex ideas.

## Mechanism (Key details)
- Requires focused attention plus genuine understanding of the why — knowing when to use the chunk, not just how to execute it.
- Built through focused practice and repetition that deepen neural traces; repetition without understanding produces a hollow chunk.
- Once formed, a chunk frees working memory slots (see [[The_Magical_Number_Seven]]) enabling transfer — a concept from one field solves a problem in another.
- **Boundary condition:** Memorising steps without understanding the underlying principle creates a hollow chunk that looks solid but breaks under novel conditions. Premature chunking during early creative exploration can foreclose insights that only emerge when ideas are held loosely.

## Application
- **When to use:** Essential for mastering any complex subject where selecting the right strategy matters as much as executing it — mathematics, language, strategy.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not chunk during early brainstorming when loose, overlapping ideas generate new connections; and never chunk by rote — the resulting hollow chunk cannot transfer to new problems.
