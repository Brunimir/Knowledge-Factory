## Core Idea (Summary)
The human brain can hold approximately seven distinct items in working memory at once — a hard ceiling, not a soft preference. Beyond this limit, processing stops and nothing additional is retained. The mind's solution is to compress: grouping individual items into labelled categories (chunking) so each category occupies one slot instead of seven.

## Mechanism (Key details)
- The 7±2 constraint is a research finding on unfamiliar information; highly practised skills bypass this limit through long-term working memory where expert knowledge is pre-chunked.
- The mind instinctively organises mental processes into related pyramids to stay within the limit — which is why hierarchical structure in documents and presentations feels natural rather than imposed.
- For information to be retained, groups must be summarised into a single new idea that represents the collection — the group itself must fit within one slot.
- **Boundary condition:** This limit applies primarily to unfamiliar information. Expert knowledge in a domain is pre-chunked and operates differently; the limit constrains novices more than experts. Also, the "seven" is an average — the real range is five to nine.

## Application
- **When to use:** Apply this constraint when designing information structures — UI screens, presentation slide counts, team management structures, meeting agendas. If any layer has more than seven items, chunk them into named groups.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not use this as a reason to oversimplify complex systems requiring high-resolution detail for expert audiences who have already chunked the domain.
