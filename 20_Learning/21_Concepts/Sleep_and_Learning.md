## Core Idea (Summary)
Sleep is an active part of the learning process — not recovery from thinking, but thinking itself in a different mode. The brain flushes metabolic waste, rehearses difficult neural patterns learned during the day, and integrates new knowledge with old memories. Struggling with a concept all day, sleeping, and finding it clear in the morning is sleep doing its job.

## Mechanism (Key details)
- The glymphatic system flushes toxins that accumulate during waking hours; without this nightly clearing, cognitive performance degrades progressively.
- The brain rehearses the day's difficult neural patterns during sleep, strengthening them and making them more permanent — this is why reviewing hard material before sleep targets what the brain will rehearse.
- Deactivation of the prefrontal cortex during sleep allows different brain regions to become more interconnected, enabling the diffuse-mode connections that conscious thought cannot produce.
- Reconsolidation occurs during sleep, integrating freshly encoded knowledge with existing long-term memories, making transfer more likely.
- **Boundary condition:** Sleep consolidates what focused-mode study encoded; it cannot substitute for the initial encoding. You must plant the seed while awake for sleep to germinate it.

## Application
- **When to use:** Treat sleep as a mandatory learning intervention, not optional recovery. Review the most difficult chunk or flashcards immediately before sleep to give the brain a specific pattern to rehearse overnight.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not rely on sleep to fix the absence of focused study; all-nighters before an exam eliminate the consolidation that would have made the prior studying durable.
